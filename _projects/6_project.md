Aqueous electrolytes under hydrophobic graphene confinements exhibit anomalous structural and dynamic properties with important implications in water desalination and energy storage. Although classical force fields and ab initio methods have allowed for considerable progress in qualitatively understanding the cause for such anomalies, the former is often inaccurate due to inadequate treatment of interatomic interactions within confinement, and the latter lacks speed and scalability to large systems.

To address these challenges, a machine learning approach was employed to develop a **many-body potential that simultaneously achieves ab initio accuracy and high throughput** in molecular dynamics simulations. This work compares the structure and dynamics of aqueous NaCl and KCl salts when subjected to varying degrees of graphene confinement.

Key findings: **K⁺ exhibits a higher degree of adsorption onto the graphene surface compared to Na⁺**, driven by its greater propensity to desolvate and migrate toward the interface. From the dynamics perspective, water present in aqueous KCl diffuses faster than that in aqueous NaCl due to the higher stability of solvation shells around Na⁺ than K⁺. These trends are entirely absent in classical force fields, underscoring the importance of machine learning potentials in accurately predicting the complex behaviors exhibited by nanoconfined systems.
